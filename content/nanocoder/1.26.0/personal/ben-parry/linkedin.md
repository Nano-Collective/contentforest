Hardware distribution follows a predictable arc. The machines developers use most are rarely the ones tooling is designed around. The gap between what gets built and what gets used is structural, not accidental.

Most AI tooling assumes access to top-tier hardware. The system prompts alone can consume a meaningful chunk of a small model's context window before anything useful happens. That overhead is invisible in demos. It is very visible on a laptop with 8GB of RAM.

The answer is not better hardware. It is better architecture.

Nanocoder's new Nano mode drops the system prompt from roughly 500-700 tokens down to 150-250. It is a third profile in `/tune`, alongside the existing full and minimal profiles. It is not a compromise - it is a different design target. The same way a hospital does not staff every ward with the same nurse-to-patient ratio, good tooling adapts to the environment it operates in.

Small models on modest hardware are not a niche edge case. They are what most of the world runs. Building for them is not a limitation. It is a discipline.

The broader principle here applies beyond AI tooling. The question is always the same: does the system make what you need accessible on the hardware you have, or does it require you to change your hardware first?
