## The cold-open hook

A small project I help maintain just crossed 2,000 GitHub stars. It has been alive for about eleven months, with no funding, no marketing budget, and no launch event. Most of the people who use it have never met anyone on the team. Most of them will never file an issue. And the 2,000 who clicked "star" did not owe us anything. They had no contractual reason to register that they cared. They had no incentive to bookmark a small AI coding tool that runs on their laptop and produces no network traffic worth anyone's attention.

This essay is not about the project. The project is evidence, and the evidence is for a much larger argument about how software survives in 2026, in an economy that has spent the last decade confusing acquisition for attention.

The number is not the point. The architecture that produced the number is the point.

## Founder thesis

The most durable systems in 2026 are not the best-funded ones. They are the ones whose architecture makes voluntary participation the path of least resistance.

I want to argue this from structure, not from sentiment. Sentiment fails. Structure does not. A project that survives because its contributors love each other is one bad month away from collapse. A project that survives because its structure makes contributing the easiest option for a particular kind of person is structurally durable in a way that no amount of goodwill can replicate.

The 2,000 stars are a small instance of this. So is most of what has actually lasted in software over the last twenty years.

## The architecture of voluntary attention

There are two ways to grow a software project. The first is acquisition. You pay for distribution. You buy ads. You run launch-week stunts. You pay influencers. You run paid growth loops that trade money for users. Acquisition is a transaction. It produces a user. It does not produce an advocate. The user is not wrong to leave when a better-funded alternative shows up. The system that produced them was structurally incapable of producing loyalty, because loyalty was never the variable being optimised.

The second way to grow is attention. Someone finds the project. They use it. They form an opinion. They click "star" or "watch" or "follow" or none of those, but they keep the tab open. They come back. They tell a friend. They file a bug six months later, in their own time, with no expectation of being thanked, because the system has made it the easiest way for them to be useful to a thing they care about.

Acquisition is cheap. Attention is the only thing you cannot buy twice. You can pay to put a project in front of someone once. You cannot pay to make them care. The caring has to come from the system itself.

## Why funded projects decay, and why local-first outcompetes

I have watched this play out enough times to be confident about the pattern. A well-funded project launches. It gets coverage. It gets users. The users are not advocates. They are recipients of a distribution event. By the time a year has passed, the team has usually noticed that the users are not coming back. The users have noticed that the product is becoming thinner. The relationship between the two is a transaction, and a transaction does not need a reason to end.

This is not a moral judgement. It is an architectural one. The system has a single input (money) and a single output (users), and the metric being optimised is the conversion rate between them. Everything that does not contribute to that conversion is, over time, cut. Including the things that would have made the system durable.

I think this matters more for AI tooling in 2026 than for any other category, because the structural advantage of local-first is not a feature. It is an alignment.

A cloud-first AI tool has a structural incentive to extract value from its users. The data the user produces has a market value. The behavioural signal has a market value. The user is, in a precise sense, the product, and the system is designed to maximise the value the user produces for the operator of the system. This is not evil. It is what the architecture is built to do.

A local-first AI tool has a different structural incentive. The user is the only party with access to their data. The system cannot sell what it does not collect. The system cannot be acquired by a third party who would like to monetise the user, because there is nothing to monetise. The user does not need to trust the operator, because the trust is enforced by the system itself.

The 2,000 stars on a local-first AI tool are not just a community. They are an evidence base for a structural argument: the people who clicked "star" did not do so because of marketing. They did not do so because the project was on a launch list. They did so because the project was structured in a way that produced, slowly, over eleven months, a small number of people who voluntarily registered that they cared.

## Real-world analogies, because the principle is older than software

This is not a software pattern. It is a pattern about how systems behave under different incentive structures. It shows up everywhere.

In healthcare, the system that pays a doctor per procedure is structurally different from the system that pays a doctor per healthy patient. The first system produces more procedures. The second produces healthier patients. The architecture of payment produces the behaviour.

In journalism, the outlet that depends on subscriptions from readers is structurally different from the outlet that depends on ad networks. The first has an incentive to be right. The second has an incentive to be loud. The architecture of revenue produces the editorial posture.

In law, the firm that bills by the hour is structurally different from the firm that bills by outcome. The first has an incentive to expand the work. The second has an incentive to solve the problem. The architecture of compensation produces the practice.

In every case, the architecture does not just permit the behaviour. It produces it. The argument that "we have good values, we would never do that" is, in the long run, a softer claim than the argument "the system makes it structurally difficult for us to do that." Good values are downstream of architecture. The team that wants to do the right thing is at the mercy of the system that makes doing the right thing the easiest option.

## The idealism argument

The strongest version of this argument says that systems built around voluntary participation will, over time, structurally outcompete systems built around extraction, because the durable form of any human activity is the one that the people involved find worth continuing.

This is true, and it is important, and I believe it.

The volunteer who files a bug at 11pm because they care about the project is producing, for free, the same work that a salaried engineer would produce if they were paid. The difference is that the volunteer will be there in year three, when the salaried engineer has moved on, because the volunteer's relationship to the project is not contractual. The volunteer is there because the project has earned their attention, and the project has earned their attention by being structured in a way that makes caring about it the path of least resistance.

This is the part of the argument I want to be honest about. It is not a moral claim. It is an architectural one. The system that produces voluntary attention is *durably productive* in a way that funded systems are not. The volunteer does not need a quarterly review to stay. The system has, structurally, made staying the natural thing.

## The limitations argument

The honest version of this argument also has to admit its limits. Voluntary systems are slow. They are small. They are not what gets built when a government needs a system in three months. They are not what gets built when a company needs to capture a market before a competitor does. They are not what gets built when the goal is to be everywhere at once.

A volunteer-led project cannot out-ship a well-funded competitor in the first year. It cannot out-market one. It cannot out-launch one. If the goal is to be everywhere, the voluntary system is the wrong tool.

The voluntary system is what you build when the goal is to be here, in five years, with the same people. It is what you build when the goal is to be trusted, and to deserve the trust, structurally, in a way that does not depend on the goodwill of the people currently running the system.

The trade is real. You give up speed. You give up reach. You give up the appearance of growth. In exchange, you get the only kind of growth that compounds.

## The evolution arc

A project at 2,000 stars is not a finished thing. It is not even a successful thing. It is a system that has produced one year of evidence that voluntary participation is, in fact, the path of least resistance for the kind of person it was designed for.

What the project becomes in year two is determined by what the project does with that evidence. There is a real temptation, at the moment a small project crosses a threshold of visibility, to start behaving like the funded projects it was structurally different from. To hire a marketing lead. To run a launch event. To start optimising for the metric that produced the threshold, and in doing so, to destroy the architecture that produced the metric.

The threshold is not the goal. The threshold is evidence that the architecture is working. The right response to evidence that an architecture is working is to leave it alone.

## Founder stance

I want to say this directly, because the part of the argument that matters most is the part I cannot outsource to the structure.

I am not interested in building a project that is everywhere. I am interested in building a project whose architecture makes the right thing the easy thing, and that earns, slowly, the trust of the people who notice. I am interested in building a project that is here in five years, with the same people, doing the same work, for the same reason.

I am interested in the structural form of integrity, not the aspirational one. The aspirational form says: "we have good values, we would never do that." The structural form says: "the system makes it difficult for us to do that, regardless of our values." The first kind of integrity is held in place by the current people. The second kind of integrity is held in place by the system itself.

The 2,000 stars are not the goal. They are evidence that the architecture is, for now, doing what I want it to do. The job from here is to notice when it stops, and to fix the structure before the metrics tell me I have to.

## Closing argument

The question is not "how do we grow?" The question is "what would have to be true about the system for people to keep showing up after we stop asking?"

The answer is never "we have to pay them more." The answer is never "we have to launch harder." The answer is always structural. It is "the system has to make showing up the easiest option for the kind of person we want to be here."

The 2,000 people who clicked "star" on a small AI coding project in the last eleven months did not do it because of a marketing campaign. They did it because the project was structured in a way that, slowly, over time, produced a small number of people who voluntarily registered that they cared. The structure is the only thing that produced that, and the structure is the only thing that will keep producing it, and the structure is the only thing worth protecting.

Everything else is downstream.
