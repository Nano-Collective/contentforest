# The commons can ship

A small open-source AI tool crossed two thousand GitHub stars last week. By the standards of any well-funded platform it is, as a number, unremarkable. The reason it matters to me is not the number. It is what the number quietly proves about how software can be built when the people building it own nothing in common except the intention to build it well.

I want to write about that, because I think the answer to "where does AI come from" is being decided in infrastructure decisions that almost nobody is watching. The alternative model is closer to working than most people realise.

## Summary

Two thousand stars on a year-old, volunteer-led open-source AI tool is a small number. It is also, when you read it carefully, a signal that a non-corporate, community-funded model for building AI tooling is producing real software that real people want. The essay below is an attempt to read that signal honestly, name what is making it work, and be honest about what it is not.

## Executive summary

- The 2K stars are not a vanity metric. They are a registration of attention from people who use the tool and wanted a way to say so.
- The model that produced those stars is structurally different from the venture-funded default. The collective is owned by no one, accountable to the people who use it, and has no extraction layer between the work and the people who do the work.
- That model has real limits. It is slower, smaller, and more fragile than the platform-funded alternative. It is also the only model I trust to build the kind of AI tooling I want to exist.

## Key points

- Most open-source projects are funded like startups and run like companies. The Nano Collective is funded like a commons and run like one.
- A "star" on GitHub is a soft signal, but in a project owned by no one, it is the closest thing the project has to a market test.
- AI tooling has a near-monopoly problem. The alternative is not "another competitor" but a different architecture of ownership.
- Privacy is enforced by the system, not promised by the people who built it. Local-first is a structural choice, not a feature.
- A non-profit with a mission can quietly become a for-profit with a brand. Architecture, not language, is what determines which one you get.
- The people who showed up to review pull requests at 11pm are the project. The 2K stars are the visible edge of that.
- The limits of the commons model are real. Slower, smaller, more fragile. The shape of the alternative is the alternative.
- Trustworthy AI is not a property of the model. It is a property of the system around the model: who owns it, who runs it, who can change it, and who can leave.

## Cold open

A friend who runs a small engineering team messaged me last week. "I want to use a local coding assistant," he said, "but I am not sending my source code to a third party. Are there any of these that are not owned by someone I do not trust?"

I sent him a link. He tried it. It worked. He came back an hour later: "How is this being made? There has to be a catch." I told him there was no catch. He did not believe me. He used it for a week, then came back: "I am still looking for the catch. Tell me how this gets funded."

I told him it gets funded by the people who use it, and by a handful of small sponsors who believe in the model. He said that could not be the whole story. I said it was.

That conversation is, more or less, the whole essay.

## Founder thesis

A small open-source AI tool called Nanocoder crossed two thousand GitHub stars last week. It has been in development for under a year. It does roughly ten thousand downloads a month. It is built by a community-led group called the Nano Collective, and the people who run it do not draw a salary from it.

I want to argue that those facts are evidence for a position I have held for a long time. The position is this: the alternative to platform-owned AI is not "another competitor". It is a different architecture of ownership, and the difference matters more than any individual product release.

A different architecture of ownership produces different incentives, different code, and a different kind of relationship between the people who build the software and the people who use it. The 2,000 stars are not the proof of that. The work that produced them is.

## What the stars actually mean

GitHub stars are a soft signal. Most people who use an open-source tool never interact with its community. The ones who star it are the ones who cared enough to register that they might want to come back. In a venture-funded product, that registration is one of many signals. In a community-funded one, it is closer to the only signal.

That sounds precarious. It is. But it is also honest.

When a corporate product gets a million users, those users are usually evidence of a distribution engine. When a community project gets a thousand stars, those stars are usually evidence that a small number of people found the work good enough to recommend, by the smallest possible gesture, to a future version of themselves. The first kind of attention is bought. The second kind is given. I want to build the second kind of thing.

The star is the visible edge of a long, mostly invisible relationship between a project and the people who keep it alive. A 2,000-star project has 2,000 people who have decided the project is worth their time. In a community-funded model, that is the entire resource base.

## The architecture of the commons

The Nano Collective is a small group of developers, designers, and maintainers who build open-source AI tools. It is not a company. It is not a foundation in the legal sense, although it is structured to be hard to capture. It is, in the older sense of the word, a commons: a shared resource governed by the people who use it.

Three things follow.

The first is that no one in the collective draws a salary from the work. The core team has day jobs. The contributors volunteer their evenings. The sponsors give small amounts, and the money goes to infrastructure and to a small, transparent fund for the people who do the most unglamorous maintenance work. There is no layer between the people who do the work and the people who use it that takes a cut for permission to exist.

The second is that the tools are local-first by default. They run on your machine. They do not phone home. They do not require an account. This is not a feature we added because users asked for it. It is the entire reason the project exists. The architecture is the principle.

The third is that every decision in the collective is contestable. Anyone can open an issue, propose a change, or fork. The work is shaped by the people who show up, not by the people who pay. This is slow. It is also the only model I trust to produce tools that respect the people who use them.

## Why the corporate model cannot do this

I want to be careful here, because the argument I am making is structural, not personal. I am not against the people who run platform AI companies. Some of them are doing genuinely good work. I am against the architecture they are required, by the structure they are inside, to operate inside.

A venture-funded AI company has three obligations, in this order: to its investors, to its growth metrics, and to its users. When they come apart, they come apart in a predictable direction: the obligations to investors and metrics win. The user is the resource being extracted from, not the constituency being served. The mission language stays the same, because mission language is cheap. The architecture changes, because architecture is expensive.

You can see this most clearly in the AI tools that have changed their privacy terms in the last two years. Almost none of them started out wanting to harvest user data. Almost all of them have ended up with terms of service that allow it, because the architecture they were built on required it. The architecture is the decision. The terms of service are what the architecture permits.

A non-profit can quietly convert into a for-profit. A for-profit with a friendly mission can quietly start optimising for the metric that funds the next round. None of these are betrayals. They are the predictable behaviour of a system under pressure. The answer is not to find better founders. The answer is better architecture.

## The idealism and the limitation

I want to be honest about the limits, because they are real.

The community-funded model is slower. A company with ten engineers and a budget can ship in a quarter what a collective with the same number of people ships in a year. Most of that is the genuine cost of building in public, with consensus, with code review from people donating their time, and with the discipline of not cutting corners that the architecture will not forgive. I do not know how to make that faster without breaking it.

The model is smaller. The people who show up are skewed toward people technical enough to install a tool from a command line, with time to read the docs, and with enough care about how the tool is built to want to influence it. That is a smaller audience than "everyone with a laptop". It is also an audience that is hard to fool. I think this is a feature.

The model is more fragile. A company with funding can survive a bad release. A collective with twelve volunteers cannot. The cost of failure is paid by the people who did the work. This is the part I think about most, late at night, when the issue tracker is long.

These limits are not bugs. They are the shape of the alternative. If the alternative were cheaper, faster, and more reliable than the platform default, the platform default would not be the platform default.

## The evolution arc

I have been thinking about this for longer than Nanocoder has existed.

Ten years ago, I worked on a small open-source project that did not go anywhere. The reason was not the code. The reason was that there was no community around it, and software without a community is, in the long run, dead. Software is not a thing you ship once. It is a thing you keep alive, and the only way to keep a thing alive is for there to be people who want to keep it alive.

Five years ago, I started paying closer attention to how open-source projects were funded. Most of the models I looked at had the same shape: a benevolent-sounding foundation on top, a corporate sponsor with most of the money at the bottom, and a slowly eroding distance between the two. The mission language stayed the same. The architecture did not. I decided that the only funding model I was willing to build inside was one in which the funding could not, structurally, capture the work.

A year ago, the Nano Collective started. We have spent most of the year doing the unglamorous work of building a community that can hold what we are building. The 2,000 stars are the visible edge of that. The work that produced them is mostly invisible: pull request reviews at 11pm, issues filed by people who care enough to file them, Discord conversations at 2am. The architecture is the work. The stars are the receipt.

## Founder stance

I want to say, plainly, the thing I think.

I think the question of who owns AI infrastructure is the most important infrastructure question of my generation. I think the default answer, which is "a small number of large platforms", is the wrong one. I think the alternative, a long tail of community-funded, locally-runnable, user-controlled tools, is possible, and I think it is the only alternative I trust.

I also think building the alternative is going to be slower, harder, and less celebrated than building inside the default. The people who fund it will be doing it because they have decided the alternative is worth funding, not because it is the most efficient place to put their money. The question is whether it is the one we want to be inside.

I do not want to be inside the other one. I want to help build something that does not require me to trust the people who run it, because the system around the people who run it is structured to make trust unnecessary. That is what "local-first" means, when you read it carefully. The system does not require my trust, because the system does not have the ability to violate it.

If that sounds like a principle rather than a product feature, that is because it is one. The product is the proof of the principle. The principle is what we are actually building.

## Closing argument

A small open-source AI tool crossed two thousand GitHub stars last week. That is a number. It is also, if you read it carefully, evidence that a different architecture of ownership is producing real software that real people want.

The model that produced those stars is not new. It is the model that built most of the infrastructure the modern web runs on, before the modern web decided that infrastructure was a moat. It is the model that built Linux, and Postgres, and the web itself. It is also the only model I know that produces tools shaped by the people who use them, accountable to the people who build them, and answerable, in the long run, to no one except the commons that keeps them alive.

I am not going to tell you this is the only way to build AI. It is the only way I want to build AI. If you want to see what that looks like, the work is below. If you want to help build it, the door is open.

The commons can ship. Two thousand times this month, somebody starred the proof.
