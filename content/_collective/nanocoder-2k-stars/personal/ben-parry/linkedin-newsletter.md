A year ago we shipped Nanocoder because we wanted to see what would happen if an AI coding tool was built the way infrastructure is supposed to be built. Open at the layer that matters. Local at the layer that affects privacy. Governed by the people who actually use it.

A year in, the answer is: a lot happens. And a lot of what happens is not what the prevailing AI tooling model produces.

## The numbers, briefly

Nanocoder crossed 2,000 GitHub stars. We launched just under a year ago. We're now at over 10,000 downloads a month, with a core team that has grown from a handful of people into a real group, and a contributor community that files issues, opens PRs, and reviews code at hours that should be unreasonable for a volunteer project.

The numbers are not large by the standards of VC-backed tools. They are not meant to be. We started from zero, with no funding, and we kept going. By the standards of that starting condition, the numbers are large.

## What the structure looks like

A few decisions shaped what we have now, and they were architectural, not aesthetic.

Nanocoder runs on your machine. There is no cloud dependency, no account, no telemetry leaving your environment. That is not a feature we added later. It is the entire reason the project exists. If the tool does not work without us, it is not really the user's tool. It is ours, with the user borrowing it.

The codebase is open. Not "open core", not "open weights with a hosted premium tier". Open. The thing you download is the thing we run. The thing we run is the thing you can fork. The contributions that have made the project better over the past year did not come from us. They came from people who saw something they wanted to improve, and were able to improve it.

The collective that builds it is not a company. It is a community-led group of developers, designers, and maintainers. Contributors shape the roadmap. The principles are written down in plain language, and they do not change based on what would be most profitable.

## Why the architecture is the point

A lot of AI tooling today is built the way platforms are built. Centralised. Account-gated. Designed so the value flows back to whoever controls the infrastructure. That model has its strengths. It is how you ship fast and fund the people doing the shipping. It also has predictable failure modes. When incentives change, the system changes. When the system changes, the user's relationship to the tool changes, often without warning.

Local-first, open-source, community-governed tooling is the architectural alternative. It is slower. It is messier. It produces fewer features per quarter. What it produces is a tool that cannot be taken from the people who depend on it, because the people who depend on it are the people who built it.

That is the principle. The answer is not better founders or better intentions. It is better architecture. Systems behave according to their incentives. If you want different behaviour, you build a different structure.

## What's next

Directional, not promised. The core team is growing, and the contributor community is the most engaged part of the project. We will keep building on the same principles. The roadmap is shaped by what contributors and users actually need, not by what would be easiest to monetise. We have a few architectural bets in flight around how a collective of this size coordinates without re-introducing the centralised control the structure is meant to avoid. I will write about those as they land.

If you want to follow the work as it happens, the best place is the project itself. If you want the long-form version of what we are learning as we build it, I write about that here.
