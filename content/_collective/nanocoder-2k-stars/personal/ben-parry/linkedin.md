Most systems that promise openness end up governed by the few people with the most resources. That's not a moral failure. It's a structural one. If the architecture rewards extraction, extraction is what you'll get.

What interests me about Nanocoder, a year in, is that it doesn't. 2,000 stars on GitHub. 10,000+ downloads a month. And the contributors who show up aren't doing it because they're being paid to. They're doing it because the work itself rewards the people doing it. Bugs get filed at 11pm. Code gets reviewed. The roadmap is shaped by users who actually use the tool.

That doesn't happen by accident. It happens when the architecture of a project is built so the incentives point the right way. Local-first means no one owns the cloud bill, and no one can revoke access. Open source means the work can't be sold out from under the people who built it. A collective structure means the contributors are the governance.

When 2,000 people press the star button, they're not just bookmarking a repo. They're registering that this kind of structure is worth their attention. That's the signal. The number is just the surface.

The product, if it appears at all, is proof. Not the point.
