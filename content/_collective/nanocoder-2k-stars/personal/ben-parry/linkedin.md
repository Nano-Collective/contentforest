Most open-source projects don't die from lack of talent. They die from incentives.

A solo maintainer burns out. A small team can't justify the time against a salaried job. Contributors show up, then disappear when the novelty wears off. The codebase keeps going for a while on momentum, then quietly stops.

What keeps something alive isn't a clever architecture or a hot technology. It's the feedback loop between the people building it and the people using it. When that loop is honest, the work compounds. When it isn't, no amount of funding or polish saves it.

Nanocoder just crossed 2,000 GitHub stars. We've been at it for almost a year. We have over 10,000 downloads a month, a growing core team, and contributors who keep filing issues, opening PRs, and showing up for reviews at 11pm. None of that is impressive by the standards of a well-funded company. All of it is impressive by the standards of a community that started from nothing and kept going.

The Nano Collective exists because we wanted to build AI tooling the way open source is supposed to work. Local-first. No cloud lock-in. No subscription wall deciding who gets to use what they helped build. Privacy enforced by the system, not by a promise.

Stars aren't the point. The point is the people who stuck around long enough for the stars to mean something. Every contributor shaped what this is. Every user who told us what was broken before we noticed kept us honest.

If you want to follow the work, I write about it in Mind & Machine.
