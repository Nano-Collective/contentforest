We changed how we describe Nanocoder. Not the tool — the description.

The old version started with "a local-first CLI coding agent." That framed the product around where models run. It's not wrong, but it's incomplete — and in a space where the most capable models today live in the cloud, it gave an incomplete picture of what the tool actually does.

The new framing leads with what makes Nanocoder different in principle: it's built by a community collective, not a company.

When a community builds tooling for the people who use it, there's no commercial pressure to create paid tiers, harvest telemetry, or lock users into a single vendor's model. The three principles that guide what the collective builds — privacy-respecting, local-first, open for all — are the filter. Nanocoder runs on any model: local via Ollama, LM Studio, llama.cpp, or MLX; or any OpenAI-compatible cloud API. Your data, your infrastructure choice, your decision.

Local models remain a first-class priority, not a fallback. The collective is actively working to close the capability gap, including through dedicated projects focused on fine-tuning small, local models for practical use.

Cloud models are a practical choice today. For many tasks, the strongest models are cloud-hosted. We don't pretend otherwise — and we don't impose a worse experience for the sake of ideological purity. The goal is genuine choice, not local at any cost.

The shift in framing wasn't about softening our commitment to local models. It was about being honest about what the tool is, and leading with the thing that actually makes it different: the ownership model.

Features can be copied. The ownership model is the moat.
