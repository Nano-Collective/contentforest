Most open-source maintenance is funded by goodwill.

Goodwill is not a funding mechanism.

The word "goodwill" appears in accounting for a reason. It is the premium paid for a company's intangible value — the difference between what you pay for a business and what its physical assets are worth. In open source, goodwill is what sustains projects between the moments when money is obviously being made from them. It is real and it is widespread. But it is not durable, and it does not scale. When the person holding it moves on, or burns out, or gets busy with something that pays, the goodwill either gets transferred to someone else or it does not. The project survives on goodwill until it does not.

The structural version is rarer because it requires someone to give up control of the money and the governance at the same time. Most organisations that hold money are not interested in holding less of it. Most organisations that govern projects are not interested in making governance optional. The result is that open-source funding tends to arrive in one of two forms: the very small (individual maintainers, tip jars, sporadic donations) or the very large (corporate open-source offices, foundations with staff, strategic investment). The middle ground — the structurally durable version — is where things actually get maintained over years, without burning out individual maintainers or corrupting the project's direction — is surprisingly thin.

I have been thinking about this for a long time, in the context of what we are building at the Nano Collective. Not because we have solved it, but because we have tried to think clearly about what solving it actually requires. The Collective has four shipped tools: Nanocoder, Nanotune, get-md, and json-up. They are open-source. They are actively maintained. They are not backed by investors. This is not an accident, and it is not a limitation. It is the architecture.

The sponsorship program is one part of that architecture.

The money goes through Open Source Collective. Every dollar that enters the system is visible on a public ledger in real time. There are no hidden allocations, no management fees beyond what OSC charges, and no profit-taking. The Economics Charter has the exact mechanism documented. The three tiers — Individual at $5, $25, or $100 per month, Supporter at $250 per month, Builder at $1,000 per month — are productised and self-serve. There is also a bespoke door for larger or custom arrangements.

None of this is unusual as a set of features. The unusual part is the structural question it is answering: not just how do we raise money, but how do we do it in a way that does not require anyone to trust us to do the right thing with it.

The answer is to remove the place where trust is required.

Privacy, if it is to mean anything, must be enforced by the system itself — not by the goodwill of the organisation running it. If trust is required, privacy has already failed. The same principle applies to money. If the funding mechanism requires you to trust the organisation to allocate funds correctly, the mechanism has a structural weakness that goodwill cannot fix. The fix is to make the mechanism visible enough that trust becomes unnecessary.

This is what Open Source Collective enables. It is not a perfect solution — no intermediary has incentives that perfectly overlap with every project's — but it is closer to a structural answer than a private bank account and a promise. The money trail is visible to anyone, at any time. That is not a minor transparency feature. It is the architecture.

There is a version of this argument that sounds cynical. Let me try to not make it sound that way, because that is not what I mean.

I do not think that most organisations that handle open-source money are malicious. I think that most of them are doing their best within a structure that makes their best insufficient. A corporate open-source office that wants to do the right thing still has to answer to a CFO. A foundation that intends to be transparent still has staff costs and legal obligations that shape what gets disclosed and when. The problem is not bad actors. The problem is incentive architecture. Systems behave according to their incentives. If the incentives are not perfectly aligned with the project's interests, the behaviour will follow.

The Collective's three principles — privacy-respecting, local-first, open for all — are not a mission statement. They are the architecture. The structure is the point.

This is why the sponsor wall being empty at launch is not something to apologise for. The system does not require the money to exist before it can be trusted. When it arrives, it goes where it is supposed to go. Early sponsors fund what exists, not what gets built next. That is a feature. The system was designed to work in that order.

I want to be direct about the limits of what this model can do. It is not a solution to the global problem of open-source sustainability. It is one attempt at one part of it, scoped to the work the Collective is actually doing. The four shipped tools are real, they are in use, and they have contributors who are paid for specific, scoped work through the community fund. That is the scope. The scope is not everything.

There is also a version of this that sounds naive — that open-source funding can be solved by transparency alone, or that removing the trust requirement is sufficient. It is not. Governance still matters. The quality of the work still matters. Whether the tools are actually useful to the people using them still matters. The money is not the point. The money enables the work. The work is the point.

But I think the structural question is the right question to be asking, and I think the sponsorship model is an honest attempt to answer it. Not by asking people to trust us. By building something that does not require the trust to function.

If the structure resonates and the work is useful to you or your organisation, the door is at nanocollective.org/sponsor.

If it does not: also fine. The work existed before this program and will continue to exist because contributors choose to spend time on it. This is one way to support that, not the only way.
