In healthcare, you don't hand your medical records to a VC-backed company and trust their goodwill to keep them private. Not because the company is necessarily malicious, but because their incentives change. They get acquired. They pivot. The board decides data is a revenue line. By then it's too late.

The same logic applies to the tools that sit between you and your code.

A solo MIT project by a single developer becomes something different the moment a company backed by Accel and Balderton acquires it. The licence might stay open. The source might stay readable. But the roadmap answers to investors now - and investors eventually need a return. That changes what gets built, what gets prioritised, and what gets locked behind a paywall. Not immediately. Gradually. In ways that are hard to see from the outside.

Pi is a well-built tool. Mario Zechner did good work with it. But after the acquisition by Earendil, the structural question isn't about the code - it's about whose incentives the project now serves.

The Nano Collective builds Nanocoder. No VC. No cap table. No exit pressure. The roadmap is decided in the open by the people using the tool. That isn't a feature list - it's an architecture. It means the decisions that get made will tend toward what serves the users, because that's who the project ultimately answers to.

Subagents. MCP support. Plan mode. Ollama as a first-class provider rather than an afterthought. These things aren't in Nanocoder because we had more engineers. They're there because we were building for the people using the tool, not for a revenue target two years out.

The principle is simple: the answer isn't better founders. It's better architecture.
