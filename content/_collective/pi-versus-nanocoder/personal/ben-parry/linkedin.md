Most people pick an AI coding tool based on features. How many tools does it have. How good is the context window. Whether the output looks right.

That is the wrong question.

The right question is: who owns the thing, and does that ownership structure make the tool's interests the same as yours?

Here is a structural example. Pi started as a solo MIT-licensed project. Last month it was acquired by a venture-backed company backed by two well-known funds. The legal code is the same. The open-source licence is the same. The team is still shipping.

But the power structure changed completely.

VC money is not inherently corrupt. But it has a specific shape: eventually someone needs returns. That shapes the roadmap. It shapes what gets prioritised, what stays free, and what gets priced. Not immediately, and not always deliberately. But structurally.

Now consider the alternative. A community collective, no investors, no cap table, no exit path. The roadmap is public and contributor-led. There is no board deciding what gets gated behind a paywall at some future point, because there is no one who needs that paywall to exist.

I am not saying the venture-backed path is evil. I am saying it is a structure, and structures have consequences that show up over time, not at the moment of purchase.

The practical difference: Nanocoder ships a coding agent that is useful the moment you install it. Pi ships a minimal core and asks you to assemble the rest. Neither is wrong - they reflect different constraints, different priorities, different answers to the question of who the tool is for.

But if you care about the thing between you and your code staying the thing you chose - not the thing someone else needed it to become - the ownership structure is the first thing to look at.
