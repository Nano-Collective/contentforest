Pi was a solo MIT project. Mario Zechner built it alone, shipped it, and let anyone use it. Then a VC-backed company acquired it, was transparent about the plans, and said the core would stay open. That is more honest than most. But honesty is not architecture. The question is not "do we trust them?" It is "what does the structure make inevitable?"

When a company has investors, it eventually needs a return. The roadmap has to support a business model. "What users need" and "what we need to monetise" can stop being the same question, and when they diverge, the answer goes in one direction. That is not a character flaw in the people running it. It is how incentives work.

Nanocoder is built by the Nano Collective, a not-for-profit, community-driven group. No VC. No cap table. No exit pressure. The roadmap is public and decided by the people using the tool. We fund development through donations and consulting, not by eventually converting users into subscribers.

This pattern shows up everywhere. A hospital built around patient care gets restructured around billing efficiency. A law firm with a strong culture gets acquired and slowly shifts toward extracting value from partners rather than clients. The architecture of a thing shapes what it becomes.

Most people focus on features when choosing a coding tool. They should focus on who owns it, and what that ownership makes likely over time.

There is no paid tier. There is no telemetry you cannot see. And a community collective cannot be quietly acquired and shut down.

The Mind & Machine newsletter goes deeper on structure, incentives, and what it means to build things that belong to the people using them.
