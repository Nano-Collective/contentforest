Mario Zechner built pi as a MIT-licensed solo project. The MIT license gave anyone permission to do anything with it. What it did not do was prevent what just happened: pi was acquired by Earendil, backed by Accel and Balderton. The MIT core is still there. The structure changed.

Zechner has been transparent that commercial offerings are coming on top of it. That is more honest than most. But transparency about a structure does not change what the structure requires. At some point, "what users need" and "what we need to monetise" stop being the same question. When they diverge, the incentive is clear.

The alternative is not a better founder. It is a better architecture.

Nanocoder is built by the Nano Collective, a not-for-profit community group. No VC, no cap table, no exit pressure. The roadmap is decided in the open by the people using the tool. A community collective cannot be quietly acquired and shut down.

The practical implication: a coding tool answers to its users, or it answers to investors. The second case is not a character failure. It is how capital structures work.

The question when choosing a coding tool is not which has the better roadmap today. It is which one will still be the same tool in three years, after the next funding round.

This is the question architecture answers.
