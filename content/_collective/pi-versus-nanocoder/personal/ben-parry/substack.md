## Summary

Every tool you use answers to someone. The question is not whether that is true - it always is. The question is who. In the world of AI coding agents, this plays out clearly: Pi is venture-backed, Nanocoder is built by a community collective, and the difference between those two structures matters more than any feature comparison. This is not a piece about which is technically better. It is about what ownership means for what a tool can become over time - and what that means for you, whoever you are, whatever you are building.

I was having coffee with a journalist last year who had just left a legacy publication. She was not leaving because the work was bad. She was leaving because the work had become structurally impossible. Not because of the internet, not because of social media, not because people had stopped reading - because the publication had been bought by a private equity firm three years earlier, and the new owners were optimising for a specific exit outcome that had nothing to do with the journalism that had made the place worth reading.

She said something that has stayed with me: "The problem was not that the people running it were bad. They were reasonable people doing a reasonable job. The problem was that the thing they were being reasonable about was not the thing I was trying to do."

That is the question I think about when I look at tooling. Not "is this good?" but "what is this optimised for, and does that align with what I am trying to do?"

## The ownership question

In the world of AI coding agents, the ownership question plays out clearly. Pi is a venture-backed product. Nanocoder is built by the Nano Collective, a not-for-profit community group. These are different structures, and they behave differently over time - not because one is staffed by angels and the other by villains, but because they answer to different things.

Venture capital is a legal structure designed to return money to investors. That is not a criticism - it is what it is. A VC-backed project needs to develop a business model that can return capital, and at some point the roadmap starts answering to that requirement. This does not always produce bad outcomes. Some venture-backed tools are genuinely excellent. But it does mean that "what users need" and "what the business needs" are not always the same question. When they diverge, investors win. That is the structure.

A community collective is a different animal. No VC, no cap table, no exit pressure. The roadmap is decided in the open by the people using the tool. If the governance is real - and that is the right question to ask - then the tool answers to its users. The worst-case scenario is not acquisition and shutdown. It is a fork. Someone disagrees with the direction, they take the code and continue. That is a structural resilience that a VC-backed project cannot offer, regardless of how benign its current owners are.

I keep coming back to Signal as an example. Signal started as a non-profit. It built end-to-end encryption as a core property of the protocol, not a feature that could be turned off. Now imagine Signal had taken venture capital in 2014. Would that encryption still be structural, or would it be a premium tier? I do not know. But I know the incentive structure would have been different, and at some point that difference would have mattered.

## What each approach genuinely gets right

Here is where I want to be careful, because the argument is not "VC-backed is wrong and community-built is right." That is a comfortable position, but it is not honest.

Pi ships a lean, composable core. It trusts you to assemble what you need. That is a coherent philosophy. If you are an experienced developer who wants a minimal harness and the freedom to build your own tooling on top of it, that is a real and valid preference. Some of the best tools in any domain started as composable primitives that the community assembled into something larger. The argument is not that composition is bad - it is that it is a different answer to a different question.

And Nanocoder ships features as first-class primitives that other tools ask you to assemble: plan mode, MCP servers, subagents, session resume, checkpoints. If you want a coding agent that works the moment you install it - not after a weekend of plumbing - that is a different answer to a different question. Neither is wrong. They are aimed at different people with different tolerances for assembly.

The real question is what you are optimising for. If you want the smallest, sharpest possible harness and you enjoy building your own tooling, Pi is a reasonable choice. If you want something that works immediately and you care about the tool you use being community-owned with no commercial pressure shaping its direction, Nanocoder is a reasonable choice.

Neither answer is universal. Both are honest.

## The dual argument, stated plainly

The argument for community-built tools is not that they are technically superior. Sometimes they are, sometimes they are not. The argument is about accountability.

A community collective answers to its users. A VC-backed project answers to its investors. Those are different accountability structures, and they produce different behaviour over time - not because of the people involved, but because of the structures those people are operating inside.

The argument against community-built tools is also real. They are slower to move. They are more exposed to internal disagreement. They cannot match the resources of a well-funded competitor. Someone who chooses Pi because it is fast, extensible, and has a clear API is not making a foolish choice. They are making a different calculation about what matters most to them.

The honest position is that both are coherent, and the choice depends on what you are optimising for. The dishonest position is pretending the question does not exist.

## What this means in practice

I work on Nanocoder because I think the accountability structure matters. Not because I think we have the best feature set or the most polished interface - we do not always - but because I think the question of who a tool answers to is a structural question, not a quality question, and I want to work on tools that answer to the people using them.

That is a personal stance. It is not the only valid one. But I think it is worth being honest about.

When you choose a tool, you are not just choosing a feature set. You are choosing a relationship with its incentive structure. You are betting that whoever the tool answers to will, over the long run, build something that serves your interests. That bet is easier to make when you understand what the structure actually is.

The journalist I mentioned earlier did not leave because her new owners were malicious. She left because the structure they were operating inside was optimised for something other than the journalism she cared about. The people were fine. The incentive structure was not.

The same is true of tools. Not the people - the structures.

When you are evaluating a tool, it is worth asking not just "is this good?" but "who does this answer to, and what are they optimising for?" That is the question that will determine what the tool becomes over the years you rely on it.

The answer is not the same for everyone. But the question is worth asking.
