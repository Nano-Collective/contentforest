# The Tool You Use Is a Structure You Trust

In April 2026, Mario Zechner's Pi - one of the more thoughtful open-source coding agents in circulation - found a new home. Earendil, a company backed by Accel and Balderton, acquired it. Zechner remains in charge. The MIT license holds. The team published an RFC explaining the reasoning and the roadmap. Zechner himself wrote a post about why this was the right move.

That transparency is genuinely unusual. Most acquisitions are announced after the fact, with a blog post that reads like a press release. This one had more substance than that. But the transparency does not change what happened.

Something structural changed. And structures change what things can do.

## Summary

When a tool changes hands, the technical product can remain identical while the structural reality transforms completely. A personal open-source project answers to its author. A VC-backed company answers to its investors. These are not equivalent - they produce different behaviour under the same conditions, even when the people involved are acting in good faith. Pi is a good case study in why the distinction matters, and what it means for anyone choosing a tool that will sit between them and their work over the next decade.

## Executive Summary

- A tool's ownership structure determines its survival condition, and the survival condition determines its behaviour. These are structural facts, not character judgements.
- Open-source projects with no investor pressure survive by being genuinely useful. VC-backed projects survive by generating returns, which eventually means extracting value from the people who use them.
- The Pi move from personal project to VC-backed company is not a betrayal - it is a structural shift. The product stayed the same. The answer to "who does this answer to?" changed entirely.
- Choosing a tool means choosing its accountability structure. That choice compounds. The tool you use today shapes the infrastructure you depend on tomorrow.

## Key Points

- Pi started as a personal project by a skilled engineer. Its MIT license and small-core philosophy reflected the preferences of one person building something they wanted to use.
- Earendil, the new owner, is backed by Accel and Balderton. Those firms have LP obligations that require returns on a defined timeline. This is structural, not optional.
- A VC-backed company that has not yet found a business model is under pressure to find one. That pressure shapes roadmap decisions long before the model is clear.
- The RFC published by Earendil is unusually honest: they plan to build commercial offerings on top of the MIT core. That is more transparency than most, but it is still a structure with a return requirement.
- "What users need" and "what we need to monetise" are not always the same question. When they diverge, the answer tends in the direction that services the capital structure, not the user base.
- Nanocoder, built by the Nano Collective, has no VC, no cap table, no exit pressure. Its survival condition is being genuinely useful - nothing else funds it.
- The difference in survival condition is not a feature claim. It is a structural claim about which behaviours are rewarded and which are punished.
- Community-owned tooling does not promise better outcomes by virtue of being community-owned. It promises different incentive alignment, which makes good outcomes more structurally likely.
- The real question when choosing a tool is not "is this technically capable?" but "who does this answer to, and what will they need from me in five years?"
- When a tool sits between you and your code - when it can modify your work, run commands on your machine, manage your sessions - the question of who it answers to is not an abstract governance point. It is a practical security and reliability question.

## The principle behind the thing

The answer is not "pick open-source over commercial." That is the wrong frame. The answer is: understand what you are trusting when you trust a tool.

Every tool encodes an accountability structure. That structure determines what the tool will do when the easy path and the right path diverge. When resources are tight and decisions have to be made, the structure pushes behaviour in a direction. You want to know which direction before the moment the decision is made, not after.

Consider healthcare. The United States has a healthcare system that produces extraordinary medical research and, by most outcome measures, poor population health. The reason is not that American doctors are worse at medicine than doctors elsewhere. The structure is different. Insurance-based payment, for-profit hospital systems, pharmaceutical pricing that serves shareholders before patients - these are structural features that shape clinical decisions before the doctor walks into the room. The doctor may be acting in good faith throughout. The structure is still pushing.

Consider finance. A hedge fund that takes a 20% performance fee has a structural incentive to maximise volatility, because volatility is how you generate the returns that trigger the fee. The fund manager may not be trying to mislead investors. The structure is rewarding a particular behaviour pattern, and that pattern is what you get.

The pattern is not always bad. But it is predictable once you understand the structure. And predictability matters when you are making long-term decisions about what to trust.

## The two survival conditions

The difference between a community-owned tool and a VC-backed tool comes down to survival conditions.

A community-owned tool survives by being genuinely useful. Full stop. If people stop using it, there is no team to pivot, no board to convince, no investor relations to manage. The tool either works or it does not. That survival condition is uncomfortable. It means the tool cannot afford to be mediocre. But it also means the interests of the builder and the interests of the user are structurally aligned in a way that they are not when a return on investment is required.

A VC-backed tool survives by generating returns for investors. That is also full stop. The tool has to service the capital structure. When the tool is growing and the market is developing, that pressure may not be visible - it looks like a normal product company doing normal product things. But at some point, usually around the time a Series A converts or a growth fund needs liquidity, the return requirement becomes concrete. What was a feature for users may become a lever for extracting value. The terms of service may tighten. The data policy may shift. The roadmap may emphasise the features that serve conversion over the features that serve the use case.

This is not a character judgement about the people running the company. Zechner and the Earendil team are acting in accordance with the structure they are operating in. The structure is doing what structures do.

## What this means in practice

I run Ava Technologies. I helped start the Nano Collective. The reason I care about this distinction is not ideological - it is practical.

When I decide which tools to build on, I am making a structural bet. I am betting that the tool will still exist in ten years, still serve the same use case, still respect the same constraints. That bet is safer when the tool's survival depends on being genuinely useful than when it depends on generating returns for investors.

The coding agent space is young enough that most people are making this decision on technical merit - which tool has the better model integration, the nicer interface, the more useful feature set. Those are real considerations. But they are not the only ones. The structural question is: when the technical questions are resolved and the market has settled, what will this tool look like? Who will it answer to?

Pi will continue to be useful. The engineering behind it is real. The philosophy behind it - a small core, composable by design, opinionated about what it refuses to include - is coherent and will serve a real audience. The move to Earendil does not make it bad. It makes it a different kind of tool than it was when it was a personal project, with a different accountability structure than it will have if it ever reaches a stable commercial model.

Nanocoder will continue to be a community tool. Its survival condition is genuine usefulness, and that condition is structural, not aspirational. If the community stops finding it useful, it will not survive. That is a harder bar than having investors who need it to survive by any means necessary, but it is also a cleaner one. The incentive and the use case are the same thing.

## The honest limits

I want to sit with the honest version of this argument, because the idealistic version is incomplete.

Community-owned tooling has real limitations. Maintenance debt is structural. A small team of contributors maintaining an open-source tool does not have the capacity of a funded company with a product team, a growth team, and customer support. Documentation suffers. Accessibility suffers. The tools that work best in open-source are the ones that are narrow enough for a dedicated group to maintain with care - not the broad general-purpose platforms that require significant infrastructure to support well.

There is also the problem of governance. A community collective has to make decisions without a clear chain of command. That can be slow. It can be inconsistent. It can produce tools that serve the maintainers better than the users if the governance is not careful. The structural advantage of alignment is not automatic - it requires governance that is actually accountable to the community, not just accountable in principle.

VC-backed tools have real advantages in some contexts. They can move faster. They can hire. They can support users with real customer service. The commercial structure creates resources that community tooling cannot easily replicate. The question is not "which structure is better in the abstract" but "which structure serves the specific use case, for the specific user, over the specific time horizon that matters."

For a tool that sits between a developer and their code - that can modify source files, run commands, manage sessions - I think the structural argument for community ownership is strong. The trust required is higher, the potential for harm is higher, and the consequences of the tool changing its priorities in the future are more significant. That is not an argument against VC-backed tools generally. It is an argument for being precise about what you are trusting when you choose one.

## The structural question

Here is what I keep coming back to.

When you choose a tool, you are choosing its accountability structure. That choice compounds. The tool you use today becomes the infrastructure you depend on tomorrow. The integrations you build, the workflows you automate, the muscle memory you develop - all of it runs through something you made a decision about once, probably on technical grounds, probably without thinking much about who it answered to.

The Pi move is a useful example because it happened transparently. The RFC was published. The reasoning was explained. The people involved are not villains. But the structure changed, and structures change what is possible.

The question worth asking is not "which tool is technically better today?" The question is "whose incentives am I trusting, and will those incentives remain aligned with mine in five years?"

That question has no generic answer. It depends on what you are building, who you are, and what would happen if the tool changed direction. But it is the right question to ask, and it is structural - it cannot be answered by reading the feature list.
