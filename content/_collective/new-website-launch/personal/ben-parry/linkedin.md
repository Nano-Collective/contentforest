Most websites argue for themselves. They open with what the company believes, then slowly back-fill the proof.

The new Nano Collective site does the opposite. It opens with the three things the collective holds itself to (Privacy First, Open Source, Local First), in that order, before telling you what any of the projects are. The principles are the filter. The projects are what falls out.

This matters more than it sounds.

A page that leads with "we believe in privacy" is a values statement. A page that leads with the principle itself, then lets the architecture prove the principle, is a structural claim. You can audit it. You can ask: does the site traffic get shipped to a third-party tracker? Does the codebase run server-side analytics that the visitor never sees? Does the asset pipeline leak browser fingerprints? If the answer is no across the board, the principle is enforced. If the answer is yes, the principle is decoration.

The new site also ships a public analytics page. The collective's own traffic is treated the same way it treats its projects: visible to anyone who wants to look. That's not a marketing choice. It's a refusal to ask the reader to take the principle on trust.

The visual treatment follows the same logic. Black-and-white, monospace, electric-blue accent, ASCII backdrop. No rounded pastel blocks. The site looks like what we actually ship, because the people building the tools are the same people maintaining the site.

This is what I want from any organisation claiming a principle: the architecture enforces it, the page just points at the architecture.

The new site is at nanocollective.org. I write about this kind of thing more deeply in Mind & Machine, linked in the comments.
