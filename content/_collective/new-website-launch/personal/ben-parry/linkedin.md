Most organisations design a website, then try to retrofit the principles onto it later. The principles sit on a poster in the office. The site says something else.

We did it the other way around.

The Nano Collective just relaunched our site. The old one was soft and pleasant. The new one is black and white, monospace, sharp edges, an electric-blue accent, an animated ASCII-art backdrop on the homepage. It looks like our READMEs.

That is the point.

A website is downstream of an architecture. When the architecture is sound, the website tends to be honest. When the architecture is decorative, the website is decorative too. We rebuilt the site around three principles we already held ourselves to: privacy-respecting, local-first, open for all. Those are not values on a poster. They are the filter applied to every decision about what we ship.

The previous site buried those principles halfway down the page. The new site leads with them, in the order they appear in our brand guidelines, because that ordering is not aesthetic. It is the order in which decisions actually happen.

Nanocoder and Nanotune each got proper landing pages. The full project list (Nanocoder, Nanotune, get-md, json-up) sits on a separate page, alphabetised as the brand guidelines ask. Under the hood: live GitHub, Discord, and Reddit stats on the homepage, a public growth page, a public analytics page treating our own traffic the way we treat our projects.

None of this is the story. The story is that the site finally stopped lying about what the collective is.

If you want to follow how I think about the architectural decisions behind what we ship (and why I think they matter beyond our four projects), I write about it weekly in Mind & Machine.
