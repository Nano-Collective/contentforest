There's a structural difference between a tool that succeeds because it was built well, and one that succeeds because it has to. Open-source software mostly operates in the second category. Its survival depends on being genuinely useful - not on hitting a revenue target, not on converting free users into paying ones. The incentive structure points in a different direction.

I think about this when I look at the 70,000 downloads the Nano Collective crossed this weekend. That number isn't a business metric. It's evidence of a pattern: people choosing tools that keep their work local, open, and theirs to audit.

Most people using a commercial AI platform are doing so on terms set by that platform. The tool might be good. The relationship is one-sided - the platform sets the rules, can change them, and has an incentive to extract value from the relationship over time. This isn't malice. It's structure. Systems behave according to their incentives.

Compare that to a doctor's practice or a law firm. When a professional is paid directly by the person they serve, the incentive is to serve them well. When they're paid by a third party - an insurer, a corporation - the incentive changes. The structure of the relationship shapes the behaviour of everyone in it.

The Nano Collective's tools run locally, stay open, and don't route your work through a third-party platform. No investors. No equity. No structural pressure to extract value from the people who use the tools. That structure is what makes 70,000 downloads mean something different than 70,000 sign-ups on a hosted platform.

The number is a milestone. What it points to is more interesting.
