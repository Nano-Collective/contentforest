# Why 70,000 Downloads Means Something Different Than 70,000 Sign-ups

Most software metrics are designed to mislead you.

Download counts, user totals, active sessions, monthly active users ,  they measure reach, not quality. They measure how many people touched the product, not whether the product served them well. A platform that extracts value from its users can still show impressive numbers. A tool that genuinely helps people can be harder to find in a ranking. The metric and the reality can point in opposite directions.

So when the Nano Collective crossed 70,000 total downloads across its open-source AI tooling packages this weekend ,  Nanocoder, Nanotune, get-md, and json-up ,  I want to pause on what that number actually means. Because it is not a growth metric. It is a structural signal.

## The survival condition

Open-source software has a different survival condition than venture-backed software. That is the thing most people miss.

A company backed by investors needs to eventually extract value from its users ,  through subscriptions, through data licensing, through some mechanism that converts the free user base into a return for the people who funded the build. That is not a character flaw. It is the structure. The investors put money in; they need money out. The tool serves the return requirement, not just the user.

This is not a judgement. It is an observation about how systems behave. If you put capital into a company, the company has to service that capital. If the company is not yet profitable, the pressure to eventually extract value from the user base is structural ,  it comes from the nature of the investment, not from the morality of the founders. The same logic that applies to a restaurant that took on a silent investor applies to a developer tools company that raised a Series A.

Open-source software does not have that pressure. Its survival depends on being genuinely useful ,  not useful enough to convert to a paid tier, just useful, full stop. If the tool stops being useful, people stop using it. There is no growth team to compensate. No freemium conversion to fall back on. The survival condition is that the tool must do what it says it does, better than the alternatives, for the people who need it.

This changes what gets built. It changes the incentive structure of the entire system. When a tool survives by being genuinely useful, the builder's interest and the user's interest are aligned in a way that they are not when the tool has to service investor returns. That alignment is not a guarantee of quality, but it is a structural condition that makes good outcomes more likely.

## The healthcare parallel

I find it useful to think about this in terms of professions where the incentive structure has been studied for decades.

In healthcare, the debate about whether doctors should be paid fee-for-service (per procedure) or capitated (per patient, per year) is not abstract. Fee-for-service creates an incentive to do more procedures. Capitated payment creates an incentive to keep patients healthy ,  because a healthier patient costs less. The same clinical decision, made by the same doctor, can be different depending on how the system pays them.

Neither model is purely good or bad. Fee-for-service ensures that doctors can provide care that insurance won't cover. Capitated payment can create incentives to undertreat. But the structure matters. It shapes behaviour before the doctor even walks into the room.

Software has the same problem. When a tool is paid for by extracting value from users, the extraction can look like good business while actively harming the user. When a tool's survival depends on being genuinely useful, the alignment between builder and user is structural, not aspirational.

## What 70k downloads looks like from the inside

Across five packages, without a marketing team, without an advertising budget, without a growth pipeline ,  70,000 installs happened because people found the tools, evaluated them, and decided to run them locally on their own hardware.

That process is not casual. Installing a developer tool locally requires a decision. You are not creating an account. You are not consenting to terms of service. You are not letting something run in the cloud on your behalf, where you can be tracked, where your prompts can be used to improve a model, where the terms can change next quarter. You are choosing to run it on your machine, with your configuration, on your terms.

That is an active choice, and it happens against friction ,  friction that commercial platforms deliberately remove. The friction is not a bug. It is information. It tells you that you are making a considered choice, not a casual one.

The number is evidence of something. That when the survival condition of a tool is being genuinely useful, and when the alternative is a commercial platform that routes your code and your queries through a third party, people will choose the friction for the control. They will choose the tool that had no structural reason to do anything except work well.

## The structural argument, and its honest limits

The argument I am making is not that open-source is morally superior. It is that it has a different incentive architecture, and that architecture produces different behaviour.

When a tool's survival depends on serving its users, it has to actually serve its users. When a tool's survival depends on generating returns for investors, it has to generate returns ,  and that can mean behaviour that does not serve the user as well as it could. Not because the founders are bad, but because the structure of the system pushes behaviour in a direction.

This is not idealism. There are real limitations to the open-source model. Maintenance debt is structural ,  volunteer maintainers do not have the same capacity as a funded team. Documentation suffers. Accessibility suffers. The tools that work best in open-source are often the ones that are narrow enough for a small team to maintain with care, rather than the broad general-purpose platforms that require significant infrastructure to support.

Open-source tools succeed best where the problem is well-bounded and the user is technical enough to run locally. That is a real limitation, not a feature we should dress up. The 70k downloads do not mean the model has solved every problem. They mean it has found the people it works for, and those people are choosing it.

I think that is fine. Tools should find the people they work for. The failure mode is not "open-source does not serve everyone." The failure mode is "every tool is designed for the same audience and assumes the same infrastructure." Local-first, open-source tooling serves a real need that the commercial default leaves underserved. That is worth doing well, even if it is not the only thing worth doing.

## The principle underneath

Here is what I keep coming back to. Tools that survive by being genuinely useful, not by extracting value from users, behave differently. They do not have a structural reason to push you toward a paid tier. They do not have a structural reason to route your data through their infrastructure to improve their model. They do not have a structural reason to build features that serve the conversion funnel rather than the use case.

This is not a story about heroism. It is a story about architecture. The 70,000 people who chose to run these tools locally made a choice that the metrics cannot capture. They chose the tool that had no reason to do anything except work well. That is not a small thing.

## What this means for where we are going

I co-founded Ava Technologies, and I helped start the Nano Collective, because I think the incentive architecture of AI tooling matters more than the features it ships. The features are a consequence. The architecture is the source.

If the structure of a system rewards extraction, extraction will happen ,  not because the people in the system are bad, but because systems behave according to their incentives. The answer is not better founders. It is better architecture. Tools where the survival condition is genuine usefulness, not value extraction, are one example of what that looks like.

The 70k number will grow. Or it will not. That is not really the point. The point is that the structure is in place, the incentive is correct, and the people who need these tools are finding them. That, more than any headline number, is what the milestone points to.
